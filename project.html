How effective is the UK's policy response to the European Energy Crisis?

Project

Aims of Project

War in Ukraine has triggered a surge in energy prices, which were already rising as we recovered from the pandemic. I aim to assess the effectiveness of one main policy that UK policymakers have responded with: the Energy Price Gurantee. It is a price cap aimed at supporting households with their energy bills especially low-income households and other vulnerable groups.

Why is this project important?

Policy needs to react now but it also needs to be effective. Assessing the effectiveness of policy helps us identify problem areas in our economy and is important for policymakers to amend current policy as well as learn from mistakes when implementing future policy.

THE STORY OF THE UK'S POLICY RESPONSE TO THE EUROPEAN ENERGY CRISIS

Chapter 1: Our story begins in September 2021, when the UK economy, along with the rest of the world, started to recover from the pandemic.

Energy prices and thereby inflation started to rise as import prices increased, a relationship shown by chart 1 to have a strong positive correlation. Already increasing prices were met with Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 to create the european energy crisis; a key driver of the cost of living crisis. Not great. But luckily, UK policymakers had a plan.

Chapter 2: The Energy Price Gurantee (a temporary price cap on the unit rate of energy extended to April 2024)

Data and Challenges: This chart shows two energy price cap forecasts, one before the Energy Price Gurantee was announced and one after. There is a significant reduction in the forecast after the EPG suggesting it might be very effective in reducing the burden of energy bills for households, as it aims. I scraped the data in python from two html pages. The link to the notebook is in the next section. I then cleaned the data by dropping rows and changed it from wide to TIDY form using the transpose function. I then saved these two tables as seperate csv files and combined them in excel to create a new dataset. I converted the new dataset to a json file in python and used the json inline in vega.

see below for interactive chart

Data and Challenges: Even though the previous chart has shown that the price cap is potentially beneficial for households, this chloropleth map shows that might not be the whole picture. This map shows constituencies and the proportion of households in them that are considered fuel poor. Although the map suggests that fuel poverty is relatively low throughout the constituencies, government data is released with a two-year lag and so current energy prices are not reflected. Meaning fuel poverty is much higher than we can estimate and therefore policy responses to help these vunerable people is difficult. I uploaded government data on fuel poverty to my Github and merged it with a GeoJson England parliamentary constituencies map in vega. The main challenge I faced was getting the colour scheme to be intuitive. It kept shading the least fuel poor areas in dark red and the poorer areas in lighter colours so I used the lightmulti colour scheme to fix this.

see below for interactive chart

Chapter 4: At What Cost?

The previous charts show a story of uncertainty. How beneficial will this really be to households and it brings us to the question of how much will this harm taxpayers in the future. The money that the government is making up the difference with must make its way back. Chart 4 has been made using an ONS API which I looped in python and then uploaded to my Github, to show net investment in the public sector. By sliding to 2020 at the start of the pandemic, net investment starts to increase rapidly. Increasing investment to recover from the pandemic, added on to the investment to counter the cost of living crisis suggests a future burden on taxpayers, ultimately making the energy price gurantee more harmful in the long term.

see below for interactive chart

Chapter 6: Conclusion

This story told through data has shown that the enrgy price gurantee will be more helpful than having no policy, and will be able to in the next year relieve some of the burden of energy bills. However as it is too difficult to estimate how many people will be affected and considered to be in fuel poverty it is too soon to say that the energy price gurantee is a success. Overall, we can expect it to be creating a bigger problem for the not-so-far future taxpayer. It would perhaps be more wise to invest in renewable energy sources and move towards alternatives to try and alleviate the dependency on the european energy market.
